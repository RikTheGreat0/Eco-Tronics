The Growing Problem of Electronic Waste

Every year, millions of tons of electronic devices end up in landfills, releasing toxic materials and wasting precious resources. Together, we can change this.

Join the Movement Learn More

What is Electronic Waste?

Electronic waste, or e-waste, refers to discarded electrical or electronic devices. This includes everything from old smartphones and laptops to refrigerators and televisions. As technology advances at a rapid pace, our consumption of electronic devices has skyrocketed, leading to an unprecedented amount of e-waste.

What makes e-waste particularly dangerous is the toxic materials it contains, such as lead, mercury, cadmium, and brominated flame retardants. When improperly disposed of, these substances can leach into soil and water, posing serious risks to human health and the environment.

Did you know? Only 17.4% of e-waste is formally collected and recycled globally. The rest is either dumped, traded, or recycled under inferior conditions.

Environmental Consequences

  • Toxic chemicals from e-waste can contaminate soil and water supplies for generations

  • Informal burning of e-waste releases harmful dioxins into the atmosphere

  • Heavy metals from electronics accumulate in waterways, poisoning aquatic life

  • Mining for new materials causes deforestation and habitat destruction

How You Can Make a Difference

Responsible Recycling

Find certified e-waste recyclers in your area who follow proper environmental and safety standards.

  • Use manufacturer take-back programs
  • Look for e-Stewards or R2 certified recyclers
  • Never throw electronics in regular trash

Extend Device Lifespan

The longer we use our devices, the less e-waste we generate. Simple maintenance can add years to your electronics.

  • Use protective cases and screen protectors
  • Regularly clean and update your devices
  • Consider repairs before replacement

Smart Purchasing

Make environmentally conscious decisions when buying new electronics to reduce future waste.

  • Choose energy-efficient and durable products
  • Look for modular/upgradable designs
  • Consider refurbished or second-hand options

The Circular Economy Approach

Instead of the traditional "take-make-waste" linear model, a circular economy aims to keep products and materials in use for as long as possible through sharing, leasing, reusing, repairing, refurbishing, and recycling.
